The problem

A spreadsheet that couldn't hold together

Cross-functional program management at 7-Eleven runs through a shared Excel workbook — three tabs tracking the roadmap, the communication plan, and the program plan where tech partners log their timelines. In theory, they're a single source of truth. In practice, they're constantly drifting out of sync.

01
Priority changes require triple updates. When features move on the roadmap, the communication plan and program plan need updating too. With fragmented time between meetings, at least one tab always falls behind.
02
Timeline changes cascade manually. An added sprint or delayed deployment requires syncing with tech partners and updating every dependent sheet. Any missed update breaks alignment across the whole program.
03
When trust erodes, the sheet loses its purpose. Directors who rely on the workbook as their source of truth begin to question the integrity of what they're seeing — or worse, miscommunicate stale information upward.
04
2–3 hours per week lost to manual sync meetings. Every stakeholder in the same room, manually walking through dependency updates that a well-designed system could handle automatically.
Root cause
"The spreadsheet wasn't failing because people were careless. It was failing because the tool required too much manual discipline to stay accurate. Any system that depends on everyone always doing the right thing will eventually break."
Why I built it myself

No migration. No approvals. Exact fit.

The obvious alternatives — Jira, Asana, Linear — would require a significant data migration effort and a chain of corporate approvals that could take months. And even if approved, none of them fit the specific workflows of my team. They're built for generic programs, not the cross-functional PM-and-tech-partner dynamic I was living in.

I was also the user. That meant I could move without alignment, test ideas against my own real pain points, and ship only what actually solved the problem. No requirements document needed — I was writing it every time I felt the friction.

How I built it

PM directing AI agents

I'm a PM, not an engineer. But modern AI tooling means the gap between product thinking and working software has never been smaller — and knowing how to direct these tools is itself a product skill.

01
Planning with ChatGPT
Used ChatGPT to structure my thinking and generate a series of targeted implementation prompts — treating it as an architectural planning partner.
02
MVP with Windsurf
Fed those prompts into Windsurf to generate a working MVP. Tested the core ideas — workspaces, dependency tracking, role separation — before investing in refinement.
03
Iteration with Claude
Expanded and refined using Claude Sonnet, Opus, and Haiku alongside Windsurf's SWE 1.5 — choosing the right model for the right task at each stage.

What I built

Three workspaces, one source of truth

PM workspace

Planning, roadmap & communication — in one place

Create and manage features with team ownership, status, and timelines. Sprint planning with phase tracking. Communication plans with stakeholder and audience tracking. Changes to one surface propagate — no more triple-updating separate tabs.

Tech workspace

Execution and dependency inbox

Tech partners see inbound dependency requests in a dedicated inbox. Accept or decline based on capacity. Mark work complete when delivered. Status flows automatically — no meeting required to communicate a handoff. Cross-team visibility shows how each team's work impacts others' timelines.

Admin & leadership

Program oversight and audit trail

Cross-team dependency graph for leadership visibility. Full audit trail of every handoff and status change. Export-ready program status for executive reporting. Directors get a single source of truth they can actually trust.

Flagship feature

Auto-meshing — automated cross-functional dependency management

Before auto-meshing: 2–3 hours of meetings per week, every stakeholder in the same room, manually walking through dependency updates row by row.

After auto-meshing: when a dependency is created, the system automatically generates the reverse and co-dependencies across teams. When a blocking feature ships, dependent items auto-complete. The system maintains its own integrity — no manual coordination required. Estimated time saved: 2–3 hours per stakeholder, per week.

Key decision

Build, don't migrate

The easy answer would have been to advocate for adopting an existing tool — Jira, Linear, Notion. But easy answers aren't always right answers. Migration would have required months of approval cycles, significant data work, and would still produce a tool that only partially fit our needs.

Building from scratch let me make every product decision intentionally. No bloat, no workarounds for features that didn't apply. Every component in this tool exists because I felt the pain it solves.

PM principle
"Every feature had to earn its place. That constraint — having no stakeholders to negotiate with, only my own judgment — forced a clarity I want to bring to every product process."
Reflection

What shipping solo taught me

Building a tool for a problem I live every day made me a sharper product thinker. Without stakeholders to validate against, I had only my own judgment — and real consequences when I got it wrong.

The auto-meshing feature came directly from asking: what's the single most painful thing I could eliminate? Two to three hours of manual sync meetings per stakeholder per week. One well-designed system removes that entirely. That question — what's the highest-leverage thing I could remove? — should probably come first in every product process.

This project also showed me that the line between PM and builder is thinner than it used to be. AI tooling doesn't replace engineering — but it does let a PM who thinks clearly move fast and test real ideas in working software. That's a capability I intend to keep developing.
