Cook Sequencing — turning a 30-second guess into a millisecond decision

During lunch peak, a store associate has 30 seconds to decide which item to load into the oven first. Five items, one oven, overlapping windows, a customer waiting at the register. I built an end-to-end ML system that makes that call correctly 69% of the time — and proved that's 40 points better than what even a sophisticated AI reasoner can do at production scale.

Discovery

The 30-second moment

It is 11:47 AM during lunch peak. Food Planner shows five items due in overlapping 15-minute windows — pizza, wings, beef mini tacos, waffle tots, hot dogs — with one oven available. A customer is waiting at the register. The associate has roughly 30 seconds to decide which item to load first before the next task pulls them away.

The job-to-be-done

When I'm a store associate during lunch peak with 5 items due in overlapping windows, one oven, and customers waiting, I want to know which item to cook first — accounting for cook time and daypart close time — so I can make one decision and start cooking without guessing, hit every daypart on time, and avoid waste.

But first I need to trust the ranking enough to follow it instead of defaulting to habit, and the system needs to explain why in plain language, not a score.

Food Planner already existed — it told associates what to cook and when. But it didn't tell them which to load first given oven constraints, and not what to deprioritize when running behind. Without that, associates defaulted to a mix of habit (30%), expiration instinct (40%), and convenience (20%). With 28 competing items, that near-random approach scores about 8.9% on optimal cook order — barely above the 3.6% random floor.

The question I set out to answer: can a learned model do better than an experienced associate making this call under pressure? And — critically — can it do better than an AI reasoning about it in natural language?

Architecture

Three tiers by design

Before writing a line of code, I made one architectural bet: this is a classification problem, not a generation problem. The correct cook order given a scenario is deterministic — urgency, demand, hold time, and historical waste patterns are enough to reconstruct ground truth labels. That makes it exactly the kind of task where predictive ML should win.

I designed a three-tier stack from the start, with a clear role for each layer:

01
Tier 1 — Floor

v1 rule-based ranker: urgency × demand density × waste penalty

Before any ML: no system. Associates guess. Baseline is ~8.9% on 28-item scenarios. The rule-based tier needs to be explainable, trust-buildable, and a meaningful lift over human baseline before we add complexity.

A deterministic priority score: urgency (1 / time remaining) × demand density (forecast / LCU) × waste penalty (1 + LCU / demand). Items whose window is closing get highest urgency; items that require many batches get ranked up; items with large batch sizes relative to demand get penalized less because over-cooking is more wasteful. Result: 58.6% on the 730-scenario holdout — a +49.7pp lift over the associate floor.

02
Tier 2 — ML

v2.2 pairwise GBM: learned from historical cook outcomes

The rule formula uses static weights. It can't learn that wings in urban stores at lunch peak behave differently than highway stores at 6 AM. It can't adapt to item-specific historical waste patterns. And it treats every pairwise decision identically when some comparisons are clear-cut and others are genuinely ambiguous.

Reformulated as a pairwise ranking problem: "Should item A go before item B?" Each pairwise comparison gets 36 features — per-item urgency, demand, hold time, historical write-off rates by hour and store type, and difference features (diff_demand_density, diff_urgency) that directly encode the comparison. Sample weights downweighted near-tied pairs (same urgency, both 0 waste) to avoid rewarding the model for learning from noise. Trained on a temporal split: holdout scenarios are strictly after 2025-05-01. Result: 68.9% on the honest temporal test — +10.3pp over v1.

03
Tier 3 — Ceiling benchmark

LLM (claude-sonnet-4-6): idealized human-judgment proxy

How close to "correct" is idealized human reasoning about this problem? If a very capable model, reasoning like an experienced associate, makes the same call as the formula on most scenarios — that validates the formula as ground truth. If it systematically diverges — that's a signal worth investigating.

Three prompt iterations (v0.1 zero-shot → v0.2 associate-voice → v0.3 plain-language JTBD). On 5-item scenarios, the LLM reached 95.8% JTBD accuracy — impressive. But on 150-item production-scale scenarios (19–28 items), it scored only 26.7% formula top-1, −40.6pp vs v2.2. The LLM passes refusal guardrails (93.3% OOS accuracy) and has better must-precede-violation rate — but fails latency (11s vs 3s budget) and fails the task it was tested on at scale. Architecture conclusion: ship v2.2 ML. Use LLM patterns for guardrails only.

What I built

Data, models, and an honest reckoning

01
Synthetic data pipeline

Generating 175,000 events worth of ground truth

There was no real store data available — and even if there were, write-off logs are notoriously noisy (gaps, counting errors, major discrepancies). I needed labeled training scenarios where I could trust the ground truth.

Built a deterministic synthetic generator covering 28 items across 3 store types for 180 days, producing 175,051 cook events, 766K POS sales records, and 148K write-off entries. Write-off quality was intentionally injected with realistic noise: 60% accurate, 15% gaps, 20% counting errors, 5% major discrepancies. An independent validator classified confidence from actual quantity differences — producing 93.8% usable training data. From this, I extracted 2,164 labeled decision-point scenarios (moments where 2+ oven items competed) using a composite priority score that blended observable features with actual waste outcomes.

02
Model development

Eight iterations from multiclass noise to honest pairwise ranking

The first two model iterations failed not because of bad features — because of bad labels. When 70%+ of scenarios have 0 write-off, a label driven by raw write-off difference is almost entirely noise. The model learned nothing.

The key insight: the labeling strategy matters more than feature engineering. Iteration 3 introduced a composite label combining urgency, demand density, hold penalty, and outcome-adjusted waste ratio — giving the model learnable structure plus actual outcome signal. That shifted from 42% CV accuracy to 64%. The pairwise reframing (v2.1) added another 13pp by giving each item pair its own dedicated training signal. v2.2 added temporal integrity (no leakage), soft sample weights, and train-only historical features. Final feature importances: diff_demand_density (0.450), diff_urgency (0.130), diff_demand (0.088) — sensible and domain-aligned.

03
Fair-and-Robust eval harness

Never trust a diagnostic set that flatters the model you like

Early LLM results on 5-item plain-language scenarios looked excellent. If I'd stopped there, I'd have shipped based on a set that was confounded by small item count, prose-mode input, and easy refusal cases. The LLM's apparent advantage was entirely an artifact of the eval design.

Built a three-tier evaluation protocol: Tier 1 (selection) — 730-scenario temporal holdout, ML only, authoritative for model choice. Tier 2 (guardrails) — must-precede violations, OOS refusal accuracy, latency budget, parse failures. Tier 3 (diagnostic) — 110 JTBD scenarios (2–5 items, plain-language), not used for selection. Added: bootstrap 95% CIs, McNemar paired significance tests, multi-seed associate floor, and a fair input ablation (--input-mode=features) that gave the LLM numeric data instead of prose — eliminating the prose confound. With fair input, LLM formula accuracy rose from 78.9% → 90.4%, matching v1/v2.2 (p=1.0). The diagnostic set wasn't wrong — it was answering a different question.

04
Demo & API

Streamlit app + Docker stack: associate-facing explanation surface

Accuracy is necessary but not sufficient. Associates need to understand the recommendation well enough to trust it and defend it to their manager when questioned.

Built a 3-page Streamlit app (scenario comparison, impact dashboard, what-if simulator) plus a Docker stack with a FastAPI backend serving v2.2 at ~100ms inference. The associate-facing output pairs the ranked sequence with a plain-language explanation generated from a template engine — not an LLM — so there's no hallucination risk and no latency penalty. The what-if simulator lets store leaders adjust store type, hour, and demand in real time to see how the recommendation changes, building intuition for the system's logic.

Agency model: augmentation only. The system recommends; the associate executes; full override at all times. Food safety decisions cannot be delegated, and trust is earned incrementally through consistently correct recommendations.

Key decision

Why the diagnostic set was lying

The most important product decision in this project wasn't which model to ship — it was which eval metric to trust when the results pointed in opposite directions.

On the v0.3 JTBD diagnostic set (110 examples, 2–5 items, plain-language descriptions), the LLM scored 95.8% on JTBD accuracy. v1 scored 89.5%. v2.2 scored 83.2%. If that were the final word, the answer would be "use the LLM." But the diagnostic set was confounded: the LLM received prose descriptions (more information), the scenarios were small (2–5 items vs production's 19–28), and the JTBD labels captured a different construct than formula optimality.

The critical test
"On 5-item plain-language scenarios: LLM 95.8%, v2.2 83.2%. On 150-item production scenarios: LLM 26.7%, v2.2 67.3%. I almost shipped the wrong model based on the wrong eval set."

The holdout head-to-head (150 modal scenarios drawn from the ML temporal holdout, 19–28 items each) settled it: the LLM underperforms v2.2 by 40.6 percentage points at production scale. McNemar p < 0.001. The difference is statistically significant and operationally decisive.

The right framework: selection metrics must use the hardest, most production-representative slice. Diagnostic sets are for debugging, not for shipping decisions. The diagnostic set was useful — it revealed that the "LLM vs ML" story was largely a prose-advantage confound. The holdout set revealed what matters at the checkout counter at noon.

Outcome

68.9% — and why that number is honest

Comparator 730-scenario holdout Modal head-to-head (n=150) vs. associate

v1 rule-based 58.6% 56.7% +49.7pp
v2.2 ML (production) 68.9% 67.3% +60.0pp
LLM v0.3 (benchmark) — 26.7% +17.8pp

The 68.9% holdout figure is honest because it was computed on a strictly held-out temporal partition (scenarios after 2025-05-01, never seen during training or feature computation). Historical write-off features were computed from training-period data only — no leakage. The associate floor uses 20 random seeds to report mean ± std rather than a single lucky draw.

At pilot scale (50 stores), v2.2 costs $12.87/store/month — 12 inference calls per day at ~$0.021 each. The projected waste reduction (3 units/hour → 1 unit/hour) translates to $180/store/month in write-off savings, a 13× ROI at pilot and ~22× at full chain (13,000 stores) as infrastructure costs amortize.

What remains open: production data. The 68.9% is on synthetic data with injected noise. A real-store pilot would provide labeled execution data for retraining and, critically, the adherence study needed to prove that accuracy translates to behavior change — and that behavior change translates to actual write-off reduction.

Reflection

What this taught me

Labeling strategy beats feature engineering. The first two model iterations had decent features and terrible labels. The model learned almost nothing. Once the labels encoded a learnable composite signal — urgency plus hold time plus outcome adjustment — accuracy jumped 20+ points without touching the features. If the target is noise, the model will learn to predict noise.

Evaluation design is a product decision. The most consequential choices weren't model hyperparameters — they were which eval set to use for selection, whether to use a temporal split, and whether to control for input format when comparing ML vs LLM. Each one changes what you ship. Getting these wrong means shipping based on a flattering number rather than an honest one.

Augmentation is the right agency model for this class of problem. Food safety and associate trust don't leave room for autonomous action. The right interface is "here's the recommendation and here's why" — then the human decides. A system that earns trust through consistently correct, explainable recommendations is the foundation for anything more ambitious later.
