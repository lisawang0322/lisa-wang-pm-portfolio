Food Planner — from static report to dynamic experience

Food Planner existed — but it wasn't working for the people who needed it most. Store associates had data but not direction. I changed that, starting from the floor up.

Usage before 7% Adoption rate
Usage after 47% Combined with device improvements
Scope 3 Tabs fully reimagined
Timeline 2 Phases: intern + FTE
Discovery

A week on the floor

My first project at 7-Eleven didn't start with a Figma file. It started with a week as a store associate — checking out customers, cooking pizzas, putting out taquitos, cycle counting, and organizing shelves. I wanted to feel the job before I presumed to improve the tools that supported it.

Through direct observation and conversations with associates and store leaders, a clear pattern emerged: Food Planner gave people data, but not decisions. There was no sense of progress against the day's goal. No signal for where to focus. And setup was painful enough that many stores had stopped configuring it altogether.

I synthesized my findings into four user personas — associate, franchisee, store leader, and area leader — each with distinct goals and different relationships to food performance data.

Process

From insight to Figma

Armed with first-hand observations, I moved with urgency. I created Figma mockups covering four ideas: a real-time sales tracker, a 7-day performance report, regional rankings, and AI-driven insights. After mapping each against impact and effort with my manager, we prioritized the sales tracker for the internship.

The decision to deprioritize regional rankings and AI insights wasn't a concession — it was the right call. Both features carried high technical dependencies and would have pushed the timeline well past the pilot window. Speed was the strategy.

Regional ranking
High dependency on data infrastructure not yet in place. Would have significantly extended the timeline beyond the internship window.
AI-driven insights
Required building from scratch with no existing model support. Conflicted with the priority to move fast and ship something associates could use immediately.
What I built

Three tabs, transformed

01
Overview tab

From static snapshot to live progress tracker

The original report showed cumulative sales by location — roller grill, hot case, bakery — but couldn't answer: "How am I doing right now?" or "Where should I focus next?"

Rebuilt as a real-time sales tracker showing progress against the day's forecast by location and overall. Added the 7-day "blood report" — a color-coded view by daypart, location, and hour that bridged the communication gap between store leaders and area leaders during quality visits, and helped associates understand where their current performance stood against their recent 7-day history.

02
Verification tab

From data overload to decision-first

The original table was structured like a spreadsheet — dense columns, hard to scan, and gave no guidance on what actually needed attention. Everything was visible; nothing was prioritized.

Redesigned as a prioritized, decision-first experience that surfaces what matters most: variance between sales and write-offs versus forecast. Did I cook the right amount? Were there missed write-offs? Associates can now read it and act on it without interpretation — the table guides them through the data that should matter to them, in the order it should matter.

03
Configuration tab

From manual drift to automated accuracy

Users complained Food Planner was hard to set up and that configuration fell out of sync with what items the store actually carried — causing forecast noise, missed items, and ongoing frustration.

Shipped an automated configuration mode: forecasting auto-enables for carried items and disables for non-carried items with zero or negative BOH. Items inherit previous subcategory-to-location mappings or fall back to defaults when no prior mapping exists. Store leaders can opt in and let auto-configuration do its job — no manual upkeep, no configuration drift.

Key decision

Speed was the strategy

Four ideas on the table. One internship timeline. The prioritization framework was straightforward: impact versus effort, and how many dependencies stood in the way.

Regional rankings and AI-driven insights were genuinely exciting features — but both required infrastructure that didn't exist yet. Pursuing them would have meant building from scratch under time pressure, with a high probability of shipping nothing. The real-time sales tracker and blood report could be built on what was already there.

PM principle
"Deprioritizing a feature isn't giving up on it. It's recognizing that shipping something good now creates the foundation for shipping something great later. Both features remain on the roadmap."
Outcome

7% to 47%

The internship pilot shipped the real-time sales tracker. Combined with parallel improvements to the associate-facing device interface, Food Planner usage jumped from 7% in May 2025 to 47% in April 2026. The metric isn't just a number — it represents stores that were previously ignoring the tool now relying on it daily.

When I returned full-time, I completed the reimagination: the verification tab and automated configuration shipped as the next phase, closing the loop on every major pain point identified during that first week on the floor.

Reflection

What this taught me

The best tool for a frontline worker is one that disappears — it gives you exactly what you need, exactly when you need it, and gets out of the way. The jump from 7% to 47% wasn't just a usage metric. It meant store associates were finally using a tool that was genuinely designed for them.

Going to the source — spending time as an associate before writing a single requirement — shaped every decision I made on this project. It's a practice I've carried into every project since.
