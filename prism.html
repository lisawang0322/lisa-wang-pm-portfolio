The problem

A workflow gap that everyone accepts as normal

There's a direction the design-to-code conversation has focused on for years: getting designs into production. The reverse direction — taking something that exists in production and turning it into something you can think with — has no good solution. Teams handle it manually, which means they either pay a high time cost or skip it entirely.

The deeper problem is that skipping it has consequences. Competitive analysis gets shallow. Design system audits don't happen. Stakeholder presentations rely on screenshots nobody can edit. The friction is consistent enough that I'd seen it called out — and worked around — across multiple roles and contexts.

01
Manual recreation is slow enough to be deprioritized. When the right thing takes an hour that should take seconds, teams find reasons to skip it — and the product decisions that depend on it get made with less information.
02
Existing tools surface raw data, not semantic structure. Developer tools expose elements. Screenshot tools capture pixels. Neither gives you something editable, labeled, and structured — the format you need to actually reason about a UI.
03
The gap is wide enough to be a product opportunity. I've seen this problem across enough contexts that I'm confident it's structural, not situational. The right tool doesn't exist. That's the opening.
What I built

An AI-powered pipeline with a measurement layer underneath

The core product takes an interface — a live URL, a screenshot, anything visual — and converts it into a structured, editable representation that designers and PMs can work with directly, and that exports into industry-standard design tools as real, editable objects.

I'm keeping the implementation details close for now. What I will describe is the thinking behind the AI integration, because that's where the product work got genuinely interesting.

AI integration

Claude for semantic understanding, not just extraction

The hard part of this problem isn't capturing what's on a page — it's understanding what it means. A row of elements could be a navbar, a breadcrumb, a tab bar, or an image strip. I use Claude's vision capabilities to reason about semantic structure rather than just raw position and size. The model earns its place by doing the thing rules can't: making a judgment call about what something is for.

Architecture

Multiple extraction strategies, scored against ground truth

I run more than one approach and let the results compete. The winner isn't chosen by intuition — it's chosen by a scorer that measures each candidate against a set of known target layouts. This is the discipline that makes the pipeline trustworthy: no approach wins by default, and the machine decides what "better" means.

The real insight

Eval infrastructure — the thing that makes AI iteration safe

Before I had measurement infrastructure, every change to the AI pipeline was a guess. "It looks better on this page" isn't a signal — it's noise. I might be improving one case while silently breaking three others.

I built a proper eval framework: fixture files encoding known target layouts, a scorer computing bounding-box overlap and component accuracy against those targets, and a replay system that runs the current pipeline against historical inputs and flags regressions. Now every change produces a number, and I can see exactly what got better and what didn't.

This is the thing I'd want every AI product team to have. Not because it's technically impressive, but because without it you're flying blind — and "flying blind" is how you ship a demo instead of a product.

Key decision

Invest in eval early, not after the fact

The tempting path was to build the product first and add measurement later. I've seen enough projects go that way to know how it ends: "later" never arrives, the team accumulates a collection of anecdotes instead of data, and every improvement is a guess dressed up as a decision.

I chose to build the eval layer before the pipeline was even close to good. That meant the first few iterations produced measurable numbers that were honestly bad. But it also meant I could see the trajectory clearly — and that trajectory was consistently improving, because I could tell what was actually working.

PM principle
"Shipping an AI feature without eval infrastructure is the same as shipping any feature without metrics. 'It looks right' is not a measurement. The eval layer is what turns a demo into a product you can own and improve."
Reflection

What this taught me about building AI products

The most valuable thing I built in this project wasn't the AI pipeline — it was the framework for knowing whether the AI pipeline was good. That shift in thinking has changed how I approach AI product quality generally.

Models are not deterministic, and that means product quality is a distribution, not a state. A feature isn't "working" or "broken" — it's working at some rate, on some input distribution, with some error pattern. The right question isn't "does it work?" but "where does it fail, how often, and is that rate going down?" Eval infrastructure is what makes those questions answerable.

This project also reinforced something I believe about where product intuition creates the most value: the problems worth investing in are the ones where the gap between "what's available" and "what's actually needed" is large enough that no existing solution fills it. That gap is visible if you've lived the problem. I had lived this one.
