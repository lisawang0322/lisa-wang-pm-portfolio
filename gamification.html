Gamification — making associates feel seen

The data existed. The metrics mattered. But the audience was wrong. Store leaders seeing associate performance doesn't change associate behavior — associates seeing their own performance does.

The problem

Metrics that mattered to no one

Store performance metrics — plus sells, reward sign-ups, reward scans, transaction counts — were consistently low across locations. The data existed. The goals were set. But the needle wasn't moving.

My hypothesis: associates didn't have visibility into the metrics they were moving every day. When you can't see your own impact, there's no feedback loop. No motivation to push harder. No connection between the effort you put in and the result it creates.

01
Associates don't feel seen. Their daily contributions — every plus sell, every reward scan — are invisible to themselves.
02
No sense that effort matters. Without visibility, there's no feedback loop between action and outcome.
03
Disconnection between associate goals and store goals — leaving potential execution untapped at the source.
Root cause
"A leaderboard already existed in back office — but only store leaders could see it. The metrics were there. The motivation was locked behind the wrong door."
How it started

From observation to VP buy-in

This initiative wasn't handed to me — I brought it to the table. After identifying the pattern of consistently low execution metrics, I framed gamification as a positive reinforcement system: turning everyday tasks into celebrated wins, giving associates the visibility they deserved, and creating a natural feedback loop between effort and outcome.

I pitched the initiative to my Director and VP, got the buy-in, and became the main driver — owning the back office surface and partnering closely with the POS PM to design a cohesive associate experience across all three touchpoints.

Scope & ownership

Three surfaces, one experience

Gamification touches three surfaces. My ownership spans back office — the store leader view — and my role as initiative driver means I'm coordinating strategy and experience across all three in partnership with the POS PM.

The MVP

Designed around the associate's day

The central design challenge: surface performance information without disrupting the associate workflow. Every touchpoint was mapped to where the associate's head actually is in that moment — not just where the screen happens to be.

First login of shift

Yesterday's store highlight

When an associate logs in for the first time in their shift, they see what the team achieved together yesterday — cumulative plus sells, reward sign-ups, and reward scans. Framed as a collective win, not a ranking. Sets the tone: we're building toward something together.

During shift

My progress tab

Accessible from the menu bar at any time. Associates can view their own real-time metrics alongside yesterday's store performance — personal context, without pressure. Pull, not push: the associate chooses when to look.

On time-out / soft log out

Soft recap splash screen

A non-intrusive progress recap on the splash screen. Serves a dual purpose: surfaces the data for associates who want to see it, and reinforces associate accountability — if someone logs onto a terminal they weren't assigned to, they'll see another associate's data and be reminded to return to their own terminal.

On hard log out

Shift recap

A full individual performance recap closes out the shift. This is the celebration moment — a summary of what the associate personally contributed during their time on the floor.

Key design decision

Delightful, not intrusive

We considered showing an associate performance recap every time they re-logged in mid-shift — not just the first login of the day. Before they reached the keyboard view, they'd see a recap of their performance so far.

We decided against it. When an associate re-logs mid-shift, they almost always have a task in mind — a customer to check out, an item to restock. An interstitial screen at that moment is an obstacle, not a delight. They'd skip past it immediately, and the data would lose all meaning through repetition.

The first login is different: the associate is just starting their day — open, ready, unhurried. The timeout splash is different too: it's a natural pause, not an interruption. Every touchpoint was designed around where the associate's head actually is, not just where the screen happens to be.

Design principle
"Information shown at the wrong moment isn't information — it's noise. The goal was to make every touchpoint feel earned, not imposed."
What's next

The long game

The MVP is the foundation. The full vision is a system where associates stop seeing metrics as tasks imposed on them — and start seeing them as a personal score worth improving. The store's goals become their goals. That shift in ownership is what drives sustained execution, not just short-term compliance.

Phase 2
Badging system
Recognition for milestone achievements — first plus sell, reward scan streaks, top performer moments.
Phase 3
Associate leaderboard
Friendly competition at the store level, designed to motivate without creating unhealthy pressure.
Phase 4
Store execution dashboard
Leadership view of gamification impact across locations — tying associate engagement to store-level outcomes.
Reflection

What this project is really about

Gamification is often misunderstood as a design trick — add points and badges, watch engagement go up. That's not what this is. The core insight is simpler and more human: people rise to meet the standards they can see.

When associates don't have visibility into what they're contributing, there's no feedback loop. This initiative is about closing that loop — giving every associate the information they need to take ownership of their work, and feel proud of it.
