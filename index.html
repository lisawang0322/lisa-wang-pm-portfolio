About me

Root cause first, always

I'm a Product Manager who starts every project the same way: asking why until I find the real problem. Not the symptom, not the workaround — the structural thing that, if fixed, makes everything else easier. Then I dream up what it could be, and I go ship it.

That instinct has taken me to some unexpected places. At 7-Eleven, my first project started not with a Figma file or a PRD, but with a week on the store floor — cooking pizzas, checking out customers, cycle counting shelves. I needed to understand what was actually broken before I could imagine what to build. That approach turned a tool with 5% usage into one running at 46%.

I don't wait to be handed problems either. I pitched a company-wide gamification initiative to my VP because I saw untapped potential in data that was sitting in the wrong hands. And when I felt the pain of cross-functional program management firsthand, I built my own tool from scratch — React frontend, FastAPI backend, and an auto-meshing dependency engine — because no existing tool fit the job.

Outside of product, I co-run Hao's House — a home bakery I started with my husband. I handle recipe innovation, production planning, and baking; he handles customers. It's a small operation with real constraints and high standards, and it keeps me sharp as a builder.

How I think

Product principles

A few beliefs I keep coming back to, shaped by shipping real things for real people.

01
Go to the source
The best product insights don't come from meetings or analytics dashboards. They come from being in the room — or behind the counter — with the people you're building for.
02
Move fast, minimize dependencies
Speed is a strategy. Knowing what to deprioritize — and why — is just as important as knowing what to build. High-dependency features can wait. High-impact, shippable ones can't.
03
Design for where they actually are
Frontline workers don't have time to learn software. The best tool for a busy associate is one that disappears — it gives you exactly what you need, at exactly the right moment, and gets out of the way.
04
Data without direction is noise
Showing people numbers doesn't help them make better decisions. The question is always: what action does this data enable? If the answer isn't clear, the design isn't done.
05
Visibility changes behavior
People rise to meet the standards they can see. Whether it's store associates tracking their own metrics or PMs seeing their roadmap drift — making the right information visible is often the highest-leverage intervention.
06
Systems over discipline
Any process that depends on everyone always doing the right thing will eventually break. The fix should be structural — automate, enforce, and reduce the surface area for human error.
