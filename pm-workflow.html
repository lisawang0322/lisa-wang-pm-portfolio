The problem

The friction I kept seeing everywhere

The PM tool I built earlier solved my pain — one PM, one problem, one fix. But zooming out, the friction wasn't mine alone. Every PM on the team was dealing with the same structural inefficiencies, just in different forms.

01
Reinventing the wheel, every cycle. Personas, interview scripts, test plans, launch checklists — built from memory on each new project, with no shared templates to build from.
02
Research that never compounds. Insights from one project rarely inform the next. There's no system to capture and reuse what we've learned about store associates, the 7MD experience, or POS behavior.
03
Context lost at every handoff. PM to UX. UX to engineering. At each boundary, some of the original user context gets dropped — because the artifacts passing between teams don't have a standard format or depth.
04
Months to get new PMs up to speed. The team's product surfaces, their ownership scope, the difference between what PMs do vs. what UX and engineering own — this knowledge lives in heads, not systems.
The shift

A personal tool vs. a team multiplier

The PM tool was about my pain. I was the user, I felt the friction, I built the fix. But fixing problems at team scale requires a different lens — not "what do I need?" but "what does everyone need, all the time, across every project?"

The highest-leverage intervention here wasn't another feature. It was giving the whole team better starting artifacts — so every PM spends less time on structural work and more time on strategic judgment. One well-designed system doesn't just help me; it multiplies what every PM on the team can do.

The insight
"The most impactful thing I could do for my team wasn't ship another feature. It was give every PM the building blocks to ship everything else, faster and better."
How I built it

System design, not just software

This project wasn't about writing code — it was about designing a system. Mapping what every PM does across a product lifecycle, identifying what's automatable, and baking team-specific intelligence into the agent so that the output is actually useful, not generic.

01
Map the lifecycle
Structured the full PM design thinking cycle into 10 stages — from capturing ideas to measuring post-launch. Each stage became a guided workflow with explicit human review checkpoints.
02
Design the skills
Per stage, identified which PM tasks are structural (automatable) vs. strategic (judgment-required). Designed 39 skills to accelerate the structural work while keeping PMs in control of every key decision.
03
Calibrate the agent
Wrote the agent configuration with 7-Eleven Store Systems context baked in: team surfaces, ownership scope, role boundaries, and decision frameworks. The agent knows what this team builds and what it doesn't touch.

Flagship: the custom agent

Team intelligence baked in — not bolted on

Generic PM AI tools exist. What they don't have is your team's context. The agent I built knows what the 7-Eleven Store Systems team owns, what it doesn't, and what good PM work looks like within those constraints.

Team surfaces (owned)
  • Digital experience
  • Back office management system
  • 7MD (7-Eleven Mobile Device)
  • POS (Point of Sale)
Baked-in frameworks
  • Elon's 5-step algorithm
  • Digital vs. physical decision tree
  • PM role boundaries (what PMs own vs. don't)
  • Handoff-ready artifact formats
Human review checkpoints

AI accelerates the structure. PMs own the judgment.

Every workflow has explicit [HUMAN REVIEW] nodes where the PM must validate AI output before proceeding. The agent generates personas — the PM confirms which ones match reality. The agent drafts interview questions — the PM refines them. The distinction between what to automate and what to own is itself a product decision, and I made it deliberately.

Issue lifecycle tracking

Ideas tracked from backlog to done — automatically

Each idea lives in a structured folder: IDEA-XXX/. The agent automatically moves folders between backlog/ → in-progress/ → done/ as PMs move through the lifecycle. Every artifact generated — personas, test scripts, user stories, launch checklists — lives within the idea's folder. Nothing gets lost between stages.

Key decision

Team-specific over generic

I could have pointed the team at an existing PM assistant — there are plenty. The problem with generic tools is exactly that: they're generic. They don't know that "suggest a solution" means staying within the Store Systems team's four surfaces. They don't know that PMs here don't write code or design UI. They don't know that handoff artifacts need to be formatted for UX and engineering, not just readable.

That specificity is what makes the difference between a tool that's impressive and one that's actually used. Every PM who opens this toolkit on their first day already has months of accumulated team context at their fingertips.

PM principle
"Generic tools exist. What doesn't exist is a toolkit that already knows your product surfaces, your team structure, and your engineering handoff expectations. That specificity is the whole product."
Reflection

What building for others taught me

Building the PM tool taught me to be a sharper product thinker. Building this toolkit taught me something different: how to design for a team, not just yourself.

When I'm the user, I can optimize for my own idiosyncrasies. When the team is the user, I have to strip away everything situational and keep only what's structural — the stages every PM goes through, the artifacts every handoff needs, the boundaries every team member has to respect. That constraint forced clarity I hadn't needed before.

The result is a system where any PM on the team starts from a stronger position. They spend less time on structural work and more time on the judgment that no AI can replace. That tradeoff — human time freed for human thinking — is the most important product decision I made here.
